Troy's Joint Task Force on Sustainability and Climate Smart Practices spent more than a year trying to get officially recognized by the Mantello administration. The administration never responded. While it waited, $837,000 in match-free NYSERDA clean energy grants went uncollected, a municipal solar farm sat abandoned, and the street tree committee was eliminated.

**The task force**

The task force was a volunteer body operating without city recognition. Its acting chair, Daniel Morisy, described the situation at the January 8, 2026 public forum. Through the NYS Clean Energy Communities program, the group had accumulated enough qualifying points to access $837,000 in match-free grants. Match-free meant no city funding required, only administration sign-off. That sign-off never came.

The task force was also pursuing NYS DEC Climate Smart Communities bronze certification, and working on an EV charging grant worth up to $500,000, also match-free, with additional utility infrastructure funding potentially available beyond that. None of it could advance without formal recognition of the task force's existence.

Morisy listed what was also lost in the meantime: the municipal solar farm had been abandoned and the street tree committee had been eliminated. Former Council Member TJ Kennedy, speaking the same night, noted that a functioning sustainability task force would need a designated city staff member to coordinate its work. That designation had never been made.

Morisy told the new council he saw the election results as an opportunity for a more productive relationship.

**What the new council did**

At the March 19, 2026 Finance Meeting, the council passed two resolutions: Resolution 36, formally recognizing and appointing members to the sustainability task force, and Resolution 37, establishing a Climate Smart Community Task Force and appointing Harris Eisenhart as coordinator. Both passed 7-0. Council Member Favreau thanked task force members for more than a year of unpaid volunteer work done without city recognition.

The resolutions would add approximately 30 additional certification points toward DEC Climate Smart Communities bronze certification, which requires 120 points. Troy had already crossed that threshold through existing city actions: LED streetlights, traffic signal upgrades, the farmers market, a complete streets code, and a greenhouse gas inventory completed through the Capital District Regional Planning Commission. The resolutions formalized what the task force had already built. The DEC submission deadline for bronze certification was end of May 2026.

Bronze certification would make Troy eligible for Climate Smart Communities grants from the state. They were the same grants that had been on the table for more than a year while the task force waited for someone to return their calls.

*Sources: Troy City Council Regular Meeting, January 8, 2026 (transcript); Troy City Council Committee Night and Finance Meeting, March 19, 2026 (transcript)*
