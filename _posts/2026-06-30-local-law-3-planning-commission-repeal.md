title: "How Troy Lost Its Planning Commission"
date: 2026-06-30

---

On July 25, 2024, Troy's city council voted 4 to 3 to repeal Chapter 71 of the city code and dissolve the Planning Commission. The vote came after two packed public hearings, a procedural fight over charter requirements, and a public accusation that a single developer had driven the change. Three council members voted no. Every speaker at both hearings came to oppose it.

The commission had reviewed 118 projects in its three years and rejected none.

---

## What the Planning Commission Was

The Planning Commission was established under Chapter 71 as part of Troy's 2012 comprehensive plan, adopted by citizen vote. Seven members served staggered three-year terms. The stagger was intentional: no single mayoral administration could replace the entire board at once.

Under Troy's zoning ordinance, Chapter 285, the commission evaluated neighborhood impact, aesthetics, transportation infrastructure, landscaping, and special use effects on neighborhood character. That authority went beyond checking boxes. Commissioners brought professional credentials, including architects, planners, attorneys, and engineers, that planning staff alone could not replicate.

The commission had approved, in its three years, the former Troy High School renovation, the YWCA, Kings Landing, City Station 2, 118 Fifth Street senior housing, and more than a thousand housing units citywide in various stages. At the Troy Savings Bank Music Hall, Commissioner James Rath noticed ADA-compliant curb ramps were missing from a renovation site plan and asked the applicant to add them. The applicant agreed. "They'll have people who can come and enjoy a night at the Music Hall safely and with dignity," Rath said at the public hearing. He then asked who would catch those things once the commission was gone.

---

## The Administration's Case

Mayor Carmella Mantello took office in January 2024. Within six months, she proposed dissolving the commission and replacing it with a five-member planning board whose members she would appoint, all at once, with no stagger. She cited project delays and commissioners inserting personal bias into decisions.

Corporation Counsel Dana Salazar submitted a memo arguing the commission's authority was limited to confirming code compliance and did not extend to aesthetic or design judgments. Salazar said the city had received calls from attorneys threatening Article 78 litigation against commission decisions. She described the planning process as "a check the box job when it comes to site plan approval."

Every commissioner who testified disputed that characterization. They cited Chapter 285 directly. Salazar's "check the box" description, they said, was not what the city's own zoning ordinance required.

The city also had no comptroller at the time. The previous comptroller had resigned in June 2024 after failing to deliver quarterly financial reports on schedule.

---

## The Developer Connection

Jessica Bennett named the pressure's source from the public hearing podium. Kevin Vandenberg, the developer behind the stalled 10-11 Second Avenue project, had lobbied council members to replace the commission. "Kevin Vandenberg threw his weight around," said Rachel Carter of Lansingburgh, repeating the description in the second hearing. Bennett had filed a FOIL request that morning for all correspondence between the mayor's office and any developer regarding the Planning Commission since January 1.

Former commission chair Roddy Yagan disputed the administration's account of the 10-11 Second Avenue project directly. The one Planning Commission meeting that addressed that development was held to discuss street infrastructure, not to block the project. Commissioners had not delayed it. Mickey Dobbin, who attended that meeting, told the Regular Meeting the same thing: the commission had asked whether the applicant had considered balconies. It never required them.

David Banks, a geography and planning professor at UAlbany who trains municipal planners, raised a legal warning the administration appeared not to have considered. Stating on the record, repeatedly, that the commission was dysfunctional or politically motivated, while commissioners testified under oath that those characterizations were factually wrong, was precisely the kind of documented misrepresentation that invites Article 78 litigation. "Shut this down," he said. "Stop."

---

## What Commissioners Said

Commissioner Sarah Wingert, a registered architect and business owner, had served seven and a half years. In all that time, the administration had offered neither onboarding nor feedback of any kind. The commission had trained itself. As an architect who presents projects to planning bodies across the Capital District, Wingert said Troy's commission was faster and more efficient than most. She told the council that constructive leadership offers consistent and timely feedback, and this administration had offered neither in six months.

Suzanne Spellen, an architectural historian, opened with the record: 118 projects reviewed, zero rejected, including 20 Type 1 environmental actions, the most complex reviews the commission handles. "That is not a dysfunctional body," she said. She demanded a public apology. She later received a standing ovation from the floor.

Bill Kaminsky, an attorney with 30 years of public service, said he was not there to fight for his job or for the commission. He was there to fight for whatever body came next. "A planning board that doesn't have discretion to do exactly what you told them to do and evaluate the characteristics of the property isn't a planning board. They're not there at all." He named what he believed the evening was really about: the desire to start fresh with people aligned to the administration. "If that's what you do, please get people, and give them the independence to do their job."

James Rath said he had reached out to the administration four times over six months, by phone, email, and in person, and been promised a follow-up meeting that never happened. He also shared an email he had received from Council Member Keal stating Keal was very likely to support the change because a new administration wants people who share its vision. Rath said he found that difficult to reconcile with a planning process that was supposed to operate independent of political alignment. Rath also noted that Deputy Corporation Counsel Rick Mory had told the Planning Commission at its most recent meeting, on the record, that the commission had operated within its purview. "Why aren't we listening to him when he tells us directly that we're operating within our purview?"

Urban farmer Dara Silberman had taken a project through the planning process and received unanimous commission approval. She said the planning director, Eric Ferraro, was already stretched to his limit. Sara Wingert said it in a sentence: "If nothing else, keep the Planning Commission for Eric Ferraro."

---

## Environmental Groups Weighed In

Rebecca Martin of Riverkeeper read a joint letter into the record co-signed by Scenic Hudson and Hudson River Sloop Clearwater. The three organizations urged a no vote. Planning bodies, they argued, should operate free from elected officials' influence. If individual members were acting improperly, the appropriate remedy was a conversation with the chair, not a wholesale structural replacement.

The letter was the only multi-organization environmental statement submitted on a Troy city council agenda item in the 2024 term.

---

## The Charter Question

Council member Vera identified a structural legal gap the administration had not addressed.

On July 11, the council voted 4 to 3 to pass Ordinance 39, which created the new Planning Board under Chapter 72. The vote happened at the same meeting where Ord. 39 was introduced. Under the Troy City Charter, no ordinance can be adopted at the same meeting at which it is introduced unless two-thirds of council members present vote in favor. That threshold is five votes out of seven. Ord. 39 passed on four votes.

The question Vera raised was this: if Ord. 39 was invalidly adopted at the July 11 meeting, and Local Law 3 then passed July 25 to repeal Chapter 71, the Planning Commission, Troy would have no functioning planning body at all. The commission would be gone under Local Law 3. The Planning Board would have no valid legal basis under Ord. 39.

Corporation Counsel Salazar said she disagreed with that reading and stood by Deputy Corporation Counsel Rick Mory's advice. No written analysis was provided.

Local Law 3 itself did not trigger the same concern. It was introduced at the July 11 meeting and voted on at the July 25 meeting, 14 days later, satisfying the seven-day waiting period and requiring only a simple majority of four votes.

Before the vote, Council President Steele moved to table Local Law 3 pending written legal review of the Ord. 39 question. The motion failed 3 to 4. Keal, Brosnan, Sorriento, and Casey voted no.

---

## The Vote

Local Law 3 passed 4 to 3 at the Finance Committee and 4 to 3 again at the Special Meeting. The alignment was the same both times: Keal, Brosnan, Sorriento, and Casey in favor; Steele, Vera, and Spain-McLaren opposed.

Vera pressed the mayor on whether the assurance that pending Planning Commission applications would carry over to the new board was in writing. Mantello acknowledged it was verbal. Salazar said a council member could sponsor a written amendment to add the language if desired. None did before the vote.

Steele spoke last before the roll call. She addressed the commissioners directly: "I'm sorry that you have had to go through this. You have worked so hard as citizens of Troy to make your community better. There was no need to impugn your reputations. This is political, pure and simple. It could have been done differently."

Casey voted yes and said he had known Mantello since first grade, that they had disagreed before, and that he voted his own conscience. He pointed to the city's housing needs and argued planning advocates were practicing a not-in-my-backyard approach.

Roddy Yagan, speaking at the Special Meeting after the vote, said he was personally thinking about leaving the city. "I've been here for 16 years. I'm an RPI graduate. I fell in love with the city. It's why I joined the Planning Commission."

---

## What Changed

The new Planning Board replaced a seven-member body with staggered three-year terms with a five-member board all appointable by the mayor at once. Mayor Mantello announced the new members after Labor Day 2024.

The structural difference is straightforward. The old commission could not be fully replaced by any single administration. The new board can be.

The commission had operated with no budget line, no staff, and no onboarding from the current administration. It had reviewed 118 projects, approved all of them, and had a written record of doing so. That record was not produced to contradict the administration's public characterization of the body as dysfunctional.

A joint FOIL request filed the morning of the July 11 hearing sought all correspondence between the mayor's office and any developer regarding the Planning Commission since January 1, 2024. No results from that request have been made public.
