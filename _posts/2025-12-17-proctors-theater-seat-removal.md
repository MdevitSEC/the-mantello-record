On December 17, 2025, photos hit social media showing every orchestra-level seat pulled out of the former Proctor's Theater, the building Mantello was converting into city hall. The images landed awkwardly against months of administration assurances about preserving the theater's historic character.

Communications Director Alex Horton said the seats would go back in and floated an "adopt a seat" program currently in development.

What no one disputed: the project carries no legal obligation to preserve anything, because it does not use historic preservation tax credits.

---

## How the Project Got Here

Mantello announced the Proctor's plan publicly on June 17, 2025. The former theater, which opened in 1914, was placed on the National Register of Historic Places and closed in 1979. The city's Local Development Corporation would purchase the building for $1.8 million, renovate it for approximately $8 million, and lease it to the city for $675,000 per year over 30 years.

The administration's financial justification: the city was paying more than $700,000 per year to rent its current home on the fifth floor of the Hedley Park Place Building. Mantello said the move would save money long term. The Troy Proctor's Foundation questioned the math, arguing that projecting flat costs over 30 years was unrealistic. Foundation president Erica Veil said: "It's not even just the fact that it's the building that it's too late for. It's taxpayers, what we're going to be saddled with."

The LDC structure was its own concern. Foundation treasurer Michael Lynch, a preservation engineer, noted that LDC ownership "places some limitations on what the taxpayers of the city can do to influence the elected officials in making decisions, because now it's the LDC, not the City Council."

---

## The November Vote

The council passed the 30-year lease on November 7, 2025, by a vote of 4-3. That was the same night Democrats swept all seven council seats in the November 2025 election.

Council President Steele voted no and said what the timing meant: "The voters spoke Tuesday night and elected an entirely new city council. They should be the ones making this decision."

The outgoing Republican majority approved the move anyway. The lease went through.

At the November 6 Finance Meeting, where the council debated the lease, Deputy Mayor Donnelly said the State Historic Preservation Office supported the project as an adaptive reuse model and that a SHPO letter had been shared with the council. Steele disputed this on the floor. The letter does not commend the project. It does not describe it as a model. Because the LDC is not applying for historic tax credits, SHPO has no enforcement role regardless.

Corporation Counsel Morrissey acknowledged that the lease contract was still a draft when the council voted on it.

---

## What SHPO Actually Said

SHPO's position was more limited than the administration claimed. The agency said reversible work would have no impact on the historic property. The city took that statement to mean its work was being done reversibly and characterized SHPO's response as endorsement.

Lynch identified the gap: "The city took that and said, 'we are doing it reversibly,' and that's where there's a disconnect."

The construction plan involves building a modern "box-in-a-box" structure inside the theater to house the new assembly chamber. Lynch put it directly: "You won't be able to go into the theater anymore because they're building a big box in the middle of it."

---

## The Numbers and the Lawsuit

Total bonding authorization: $12.5 million, with a guaranteed maximum price of $10.6 million, coming in about $1 million under the original projection. Council President Steele flagged a $1.6 million furniture purchase at the December 17 meeting where the seat photos circulated.

The LDC received the deed on December 23, 2025, six days after the seat photos appeared. Mantello's target opening date: January 1, 2027.

The Troy Proctor's Foundation filed a 91-page lawsuit against the LDC on February 14, 2026. The suit alleged SEQRA violations, insufficient environmental review, and irreversible damage to the building's historic character. It also flagged undisclosed material risks: asbestos, lead paint, and 1914-era steel beams. The foundation sought to halt all construction until a proper review was completed.

---

**Partisan angle:** The lease passed 4-3 on the night the voters replaced the majority that approved it. The administration told the council SHPO endorsed the project. SHPO said reversible work has no impact; the administration called that an endorsement. The seats were pulled before the new council was sworn in.
