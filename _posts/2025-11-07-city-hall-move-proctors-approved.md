Four to three, straight down party lines. The Republican-majority council approved the Proctor's Theater lease on November 6 and 7, 2025, two months before the new Democratic supermajority would take office. Four new Democrats had won election two days earlier. Council President Steele argued the incoming council should make that call. She was outvoted. The Troy Local Development Corporation approved up to $12.5 million in tax-exempt bonds for the project, with the city paying $675,000 per year for 30 years.

**The terms**

Total project cost: approximately $11.1 million, covering $1.8 million for building acquisition, $8.1 million for construction, and the remainder in capitalized interest, legal, and finance costs. The lease is triple-net in form, making the city responsible for taxes, utilities, insurance, and maintenance, though Donnelly said the LDC committed to carry building insurance and not pass that cost to the city. The fixed rent is $675,000 per year, capped at $685,000. Projected savings against the current city hall lease: $112,000 per year by 2027, $213,000 by 2033, and $422,000 by 2043.

The comparison relied on the current lease rate of $751,000 with a 3 percent annual escalator. Council Member Vera pointed out on the floor that this administration added the escalator when it renegotiated the prior lease. The original 2012-2022 city hall lease had no escalator. The savings projection was built against terms this administration had itself created.

**The construction plan**

The disputed centerpiece of the project is a new assembly hall inside the theater, a structure built on lightweight steel framing on 2-inch concrete over structural foam, installed inside the auditorium. The administration calls it a "box within a box." John White, an architectural preservationist who testified at the November 6 Finance Meeting, said the plan would destroy the lower 15 feet of the auditorium, damage the stage, the orchestra floor, and four of the five arches, and violate Secretary of Interior standards. He called the $8 million construction estimate implausible given a 2010 assessment putting restoration at $10 million. Daniel Moresy, an architect from the Sycaway neighborhood, said the work would irreversibly destroy character-defining features.

The administration's answer to the preservation question was that the stage was already compromised, no arches would be removed, and the curtain would be stored. On cost, Deputy Mayor Donnelly pointed to a guaranteed maximum price construction contract, meaning the contractor absorbs overruns up to the GMP.

**SHPO and the letter**

Donnelly said the State Historic Preservation Office supports the project as an adaptive reuse model and that a SHPO letter had been shared with the council. Council President Steele disputed the characterization directly: the letter does not commend the project, she said, and does not describe it as a model. She also noted that since the LDC is not seeking historic tax credits, SHPO has no enforcement role regardless of what the letter says.

**The process argument**

Three of the four no votes shared a common complaint: the administration withheld project documents for months while asking the council to vote on an $11 million commitment. Steele said she had written to the administration in June and every month after that requesting basic project information. She never received a reply. Her position: the outgoing council had no right to bind an incoming council and the public to a 30-year lease without releasing the underlying documents.

Council Member Casey closed the debate with a different theory of the election results from two days earlier. "The November 4 election results were a national anti-Republican wave," he said, not a referendum on the city hall proposal.

**The same night**

Also at the November 6 Finance Meeting, Corporation Counsel Morrissey announced a tentative $134,000 settlement with First Columbia Management over disputed common area maintenance charges for the current city hall lease. Legislation to formalize the settlement came before the council at the November 20 Finance Meeting.

**Partisan angle:** The vote happened when it did because the administration wanted the city hall move locked in before the new Democratic supermajority took office in January 2026. The incoming council members had no vote on a 30-year commitment that will define city finances through 2055.
